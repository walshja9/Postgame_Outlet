## /pages/methodology-preview — "Methodology"

**Page title:** Methodology

Postgame Outlet publishes one flagship number for every NFL team: a Power
Rating. This page explains what that number means and how it is set, in plain
language.

**What a Power Rating is.** A Power Rating estimates how many points stronger
or weaker a team is than a league-average team on a neutral field. A team rated
+7.0 would be favored by about a touchdown over a perfectly average opponent at
a neutral site. A team at −3.0 would be about a field goal worse than average.
Zero is average, by construction.

**How the number is built.** Each team's rating is the straight sum of three
parts, so the board always reconciles: a quarterback value, a non-QB offense
value, and a defense value. The quarterback is the dominant lever — elite
starters are worth five points or more over an average starter, and the worst
starters cost their team real points. Offense and defense capture the rest of
the roster, centered at average.

**Where the inputs come from.** Ratings start from last season's results,
blended with a roster-based build that prices offseason movement — free agency,
the draft, coaching changes, and injuries. Early in the year the two are
weighted about evenly; as real games arrive, results take over. Teams whose
previous season ended with an injured or benched starter are weighted toward
roster talent rather than a misleading final record.

**Home field.** When ratings are used to think about a specific game, home
field is worth about a point and a half on average — more in the hardest
buildings, less in the friendliest ones, with a small additional edge for
prime-time home games.

**These are living numbers.** Ratings move when new information arrives:
injuries, trades, performance. Every published edition is dated, attributed to
its author, and preserved as a permanent snapshot — see our
[Corrections and accountability](/pages/accountability-preview) policy.

**What ratings are not.** A Power Rating measures season-long team strength.
It is not a prediction for any single game, and it does not account for
motivation, rest, or matchup-specific wrinkles. Postgame Outlet keeps team
ratings and single-game analysis clearly separated, and labels each.

## /pages/accountability-preview — "Corrections & Accountability"

**Page title:** Corrections & Accountability

Analysis is only worth trusting if you can check it later. These are the rules
every Postgame Outlet number and claim follows.

**Everything published is named, dated, and versioned.** Ratings editions and
analysis articles carry a named author, a publication timestamp, an update
timestamp when applicable, and the version of the model or data behind them.

**Nothing publishes without review.** Work that has not passed editorial
review does not appear on this site. Our tooling enforces this: unreviewed
ratings physically cannot be generated into public output.

**Published numbers are locked.** When a ratings edition is published, it
becomes a permanent snapshot. Later editions are new snapshots — the old ones
stay visible. We do not silently rewrite history.

**Corrections are disclosed, not disappeared.** When we get something wrong,
the original stays up and a dated correction note explains what changed and
why. Articles carry their correction history at the bottom of the page.

**Results stay on the record.** Wins and losses on our published calls remain
equally visible. Historical editions are archived, labeled, and linked from the
current ratings — the receipts don't expire.

**External data is sourced or absent.** Any external benchmark we show carries
its source and capture time. If a data source is unavailable, we say
"unavailable" — we never fabricate a value.

Questions about a specific number or correction: use the
[contact page](/pages/contact).
